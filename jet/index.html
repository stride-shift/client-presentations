Phase 1 options

Four ways to start. One recommendation.

Each option is scoped to deliver in 2–4 weeks. No UI to log into — these are agentic tools that produce outputs JET can use immediately, and that feed into whatever business system they select.

A
Deal Qualification Engine Recommended
Codify the go/no-go decision — the highest-leverage point in the pipeline
What it does: Takes a grant or tender opportunity (URL, PDF, or pasted text) and automatically:
  • Extracts key parameters: funder, budget range, deadline, sector, geographic focus, evaluation criteria
  • Researches the funder: past awards, stated and unstated biases (HEIs vs. NGOs?), budget patterns, governance risks
  • Scores the opportunity against a qualification rubric built from JET's profile, history, and strategic priorities
  • Classifies pricing sensitivity: is this a deep-pockets innovator or a budget-constrained NGO?
  • Outputs a go/no-go recommendation with reasoning, suggested team, and pricing guidance

Why this is the starting point: James said the go/no-go step "is definitely not codified." Carla said they lose proposals to funder biases they didn't research upfront. Every minute spent on a doomed proposal is time a senior expert isn't spending on a winnable one. This option directly targets the constraint — senior time — at the moment it matters most.

How we'd validate it: Run it against 15–20 of JET's past opportunities (won and lost). Show it would have correctly flagged the ones they lost on price or funder bias. If it can retrospectively call the no-go's, it's already paying for itself.

What it doesn't need: No UI. No integration with the business system. It produces a structured report per opportunity. Carla can run it when an opportunity comes in, before the email to James.

B
CV Tailoring Agent Recommended
Auto-generate role-specific CVs for every proposal, every time
What it does: Maintains a living knowledge base of each JET team member's full profile — qualifications, publications, project history, skills, and experience. When given a specific tender:
  • Identifies which team members are the best fit for which roles
  • Generates a tailored CV per person that emphasises the relevant experience for that scope
  • Formats to the funder's specific template requirements
  • Updates the knowledge base as new projects complete and new qualifications are earned

Why this one matters: Carla said she'd "love you forever" if this was solved. It's the most emotionally resonant pain point and also the most contained: clear input (master CV + tender), clear output (tailored CVs), easy to evaluate quality. It directly frees senior time by eliminating a task that currently requires expert judgement to match person-to-role.

How we'd validate it: Take 3–5 team members' existing CVs and a recent tender. Show the before (generic CV) vs. after (tailored CV) side by side. Carla reviews whether she'd submit them.

What it doesn't need: No integration with HR systems. JET provides master CVs (Word, PDF, whatever they have). The agent does the rest.

C
Opportunity Scanner + Classifier
Continuous monitoring of global grants, ranked by JET-fit
What it does: Continuously monitors grant databases, donor portals, and government procurement sites (South African and international). For each opportunity:
  • Classifies by sector fit: education, TVET, ECD, micro-credentials, AI in education, mental health (adjacent)
  • Estimates budget range and price sensitivity
  • Flags deadline, competition level, and whether it suits JET's longitudinal-project preference
  • Delivers a weekly digest of qualified opportunities, priority-ranked

Why it's valuable: Jaya raised this directly — they find grants too late. Being first to know is a structural advantage, especially for opportunities where turnaround time is tight and competitors haven't started yet.

Why it's medium-effort: Requires setting up monitoring infrastructure across multiple sources. Coverage is hard to validate unless we can show retrospectively that it would have surfaced opportunities JET missed in the last 6–12 months.

Natural pairing: Feeds directly into Option A. The scanner finds, the qualification engine filters. Together they form a complete early pipeline.

D
Proposal Draft Accelerator
First-draft proposals generated from JET's knowledge base + tender specs
What it does: Given a qualified opportunity, generates a first-draft proposal by drawing on JET's archive of past proposals, methodologies, and sector expertise:
  • Structures the response to the funder's required template
  • Drafts methodology, approach, and theory of change sections using JET's established frameworks
  • Pre-populates team composition (integrates with Option B's CV knowledge base)
  • Generates a budget framework based on the funder's stated range and JET's cost structures
  • Senior team reviews and refines rather than writing from scratch

Why it's the big unlock: This is where the "10 proposals at the same quality, not 100 proposals at lower quality" vision lives. If the draft quality is high enough, senior QA becomes genuinely just review — not rewriting.

Why it's phase 2: Requires ingesting JET's proposal archive and building a deep understanding of their methodologies. It's a larger data onboarding exercise and the output quality is harder to validate quickly. Options A and B build the trust and data foundation this needs.

Where the internal pushback lives: This is where "we can do this ourselves with ChatGPT" sounds most plausible. The difference is between generic AI-written proposals and proposals that encode JET's 30 years of institutional knowledge — but that difference is hard to demonstrate without the data onboarding.

Our recommendation

Start with A + B together.

Two tools, two pain points, one phase. They bookend the early pipeline without touching proposal writing — which is where the "we can do this ourselves" pushback lives.

Why these two.
Option A (Deal Qualification) targets the highest-leverage decision: should you spend time on this at all? Every hour saved on a doomed proposal is an hour a senior expert gets back for a winnable one. It's the first thing Carla does when an opportunity arrives — and right now it's an email chain, not a system.

Option B (CV Tailoring) is the most contained deliverable with the clearest "wow" moment. Clear input, clear output, immediately evaluable. Carla validates it by looking at the CVs and deciding whether she'd submit them. No ambiguity.

Together they answer two questions per opportunity: "Should we go?" and "Here's the team, ready to submit." Neither requires a UI — both produce documents. Neither overlaps with the business system RFP. Both can be validated against real JET history within the first week.
What Phase 1 unlocks
➔
Option C becomes natural.
Once you have a qualification rubric (A), plugging a scanner (C) into it is straightforward. The rubric defines what to look for; the scanner finds it.
➔
Option D becomes possible.
The CV knowledge base (B) and qualification intelligence (A) are the foundation the proposal accelerator needs. Phase 1 builds that foundation while delivering standalone value.
➔
Trust is established.
James needs to sell this internally. Retrospective validation ("the system would have flagged these 5 proposals as no-go, and you lost all 5") is the most powerful proof point for that conversation.
➔
No UI, no overlap.
Both tools are agentic — they produce documents, not dashboards. They complement whatever business system JET selects from their RFP, rather than competing with it.
