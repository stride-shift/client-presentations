StrideShift x JET Education Services

"Can't we just do
this ourselves?"

Four reasons it's harder than it sounds.

1
You can't see the problem you actually have.
We walked into this conversation asking for a grants engine. Within thirty minutes, we'd uncovered that we're mostly not losing on proposal quality — we're losing on price. That reframe changes everything: the solution isn't "write better proposals," it's "know which opportunities are actually winnable before investing weeks."

That insight didn't come from inside. It came from someone asking questions we hadn't thought to ask ourselves.
"The usual reason we do not win is not quality. In fact they love our proposals. But they can't afford us."
— Carla, in the discovery session
2
The people who'd build it are the bottleneck.
Our senior team is already stretched across 10 things. They're the constraint on every proposal we write — QA, methodology, budgets, sign-off. Asking those same people to also architect an AI system means pulling them off the revenue-generating work that keeps us running.

We can't redesign the engine while it's the only thing keeping the car moving.
"Our reliance on our senior team is high. We've got enough middle and junior people that can do stuff, but the reliance on that senior person that's doing 10 things — it becomes a problem."
— James, in the discovery session
3
Prompting ChatGPT is not a system.
Yes, anyone can ask an AI to draft a proposal section. But individual prompting gives you individual results — inconsistent, unrepeatable, and disconnected from our institutional knowledge.

A deal qualification rubric that encodes 30 years of JET's decision-making is fundamentally different from someone pasting a tender into ChatGPT and asking "should we pursue this?" The first is a system. The second is a shortcut.
"This is not about going to ChatGPT and prompting. We actually have to figure out what intelligence looks like for you and where you should be smarter."
— Alison, in the discovery session
4
The ground is shifting beneath us.
We've succeeded for 30 years by being excellent at research, M&E, and implementation in education. But the cost structure funders expect is changing. When competitors can do with 2 people and AI what used to take 20, our pricing model — however fair — starts looking expensive.

Adapting to that isn't a weekend project. It requires thinking through our positioning, our pricing, and where AI fits into how we work — not just bolting a chatbot onto our current process.
"No one's going to pay for a team of 20 researchers when they can see that people are doing it with one person and 100 agents."
— Alison, in the discovery session

We're not buying software.
We're buying thinking we don't have time to do.

The value isn't in tools we could theoretically build. It's in the diagnostic work, the reframing, and the speed of someone who does this full-time — so our experts can stay focused on what they're best at.
